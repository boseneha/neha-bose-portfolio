Selected case studies

Three ways in, one question underneath

Where does trust break, and how would you know early enough to act. Each study below took a different method because the question demanded it.

Catching account-recovery fraud in the conversation itself

Mixed-methods / computational qualitative research . Meta, Trust and Safety

Problem and framing

When someone is locked out of their account, they can reach a human agent to recover it. Attackers exploit that same path, socially engineering their way into accounts they do not own. The existing defence was retrospective, reviewers read transcripts after the fact, once the account had already changed hands, and could only ever sample a fraction. I set out to answer a sharper question: is there a signal in the conversation itself that separates a genuinely locked-out user from an attacker, early enough to act on.

Approach and methods

This needed a qualitative-to-quantitative loop, not one or the other. The discriminating behaviour was unknown and human, a qualitative discovery problem, but to be trustworthy and scalable it had to be validated statistically and shipped as code. So I designed a four-stage pipeline: LLM-assisted signal discovery, semantic de-duplication, expert consolidation into precise testable definitions, and quantitative validation.

Process

I used a large language model to read matched sets of confirmed-fraud and legitimate transcripts and surface candidate behavioural patterns, clustered hundreds of raw candidates down to a stable set, rewrote each as an explicit binary scoring rule, then scored the full corpus and tested every signal for statistical significance. Rigour sat at both ends: discovery grounded in human-labelled ground truth and structured to compare like with like, validation via significance testing and a held-out classifier, checking train-versus-test performance to confirm the model generalised rather than memorised.

Figure 1. The discovery pipeline narrowed many candidate signals to a validated few. Illustrative, synthetic data.

Insight

Fraudulent and legitimate recovery conversations do diverge in consistent, measurable ways, and those behavioural patterns held up on unseen data, enough for a model an engineering team would trust in production. The specific signals and model internals are withheld here for security reasons.

Figure 2. How sharply each behavioural signal separated the groups. Illustrative, synthetic data, signal names are placeholders.
Figure 3. Model performance, ROC curve. Illustrative, synthetic data.
Figure 4. A tiered risk output lets operations choose the trade-off. Illustrative, synthetic data.

Impact, what moved

Detection shifted from retrospective, manual and low-coverage to automated behavioural scoring running in production across every conversation. It became a staffed item on the team's roadmap, and it opened the path the team is now pursuing, moving from catching fraud after the account is handed over to intervening during the conversation, before it is.

Where account recovery quietly fails, even when it succeeds

Large-scale on-platform survey . quantitative . Meta, Account Access

Problem and framing

Regaining access to a locked account runs through a fragmented set of recovery flows, often owned by different teams, and users hit a complex mix of them. Most prior research looked at a single flow, or at outright failures. I reframed the question: instead of studying failures, study successful recoveries across many flows to find where success is fragile, because pain points that merely slow down people who do recover are the same ones that make others abandon entirely.

Approach and methods

A single-flow study cannot capture experience that varies by entry point, eligibility, device and situation. So I ran a cross-flow on-platform survey of users who had recently and successfully recovered across six high-volume recovery methods, a comparative design that surfaces which flows are fragile relative to the others. Successful recoverers are also an easier population to reach, and their friction is a leading indicator of others' failure.

Process

I designed the screener and questionnaire to isolate genuine recent recoverers, route them by flow, and measure four things comparably across flows: recovery time, difficulties encountered, post-recovery protective actions, and perceived ease. Analysis was comparative, the point was the contrast between flows, not any one in isolation.

Insight: ease is not time

The most difficult-rated flow was not the most time-consuming. Getting stuck, a verification code that will not work, an identity check with no response, makes a fast flow feel harder than a genuinely long one. Difficulty is driven by friction and uncertainty, not duration. That reframed where to invest: fix the stuck moments, not just the slow ones.

Figure 1. Perceived difficulty versus perceived time by recovery method. Illustrative, synthetic data, labels are generic method types.

Supporting findings

Code-delivery and identity-verification steps drove the most reported friction, and protective actions after recovery were strikingly low, many users took none, leaving them exposed to repeat compromise.

Figure 2. Most-reported recovery pain points. Illustrative, synthetic data.
Figure 3. Protective actions taken after recovery. Illustrative, synthetic data.

Impact, what moved

  • Recommendations adopted into the product backlog and team priorities, targeting the highest-friction verification steps and the stuck moments rather than raw time.
  • Reframed the team's mental model from how long does recovery take to where does recovery feel fragile, a more actionable lens for reducing preventable churn.
  • Proposed a logged proxy for recovery-experience quality, repeated challenge attempts and time-to-clear per step, so the team could track improvement over time, turning a one-off study into an ongoing measure.

How businesses lose control of their accounts

Qualitative research . in-depth interviews and thematic analysis . Meta, Trust and Safety

Problem and framing

Business-account takeover often begins not with a clever exploit but with weak everyday access management, too many admins, access never revoked, no clear view of who can do what. The team could see the risk in the abstract, but not the behaviour behind it. I set out to understand how businesses actually grant, share and lose track of account access, the mental models and habits that quietly open the door to compromise.

Approach and methods

This is a why question about human behaviour and context, so it called for depth, not scale: in-depth interviews with the people who live it, business owners, agency staff and admins, across multiple markets to capture how practice varies. I analysed the transcripts through thematic analysis, coding what people said and clustering it via affinity mapping into a small set of themes that explained the risk.

Process: thematic analysis

I coded every transcript, then used affinity mapping to cluster hundreds of individual observations into coherent themes, moving from raw quotes to patterns to a structured account of how access management fails in practice.

Figure 1. Affinity mapping, clustering observations into themes. Illustrative, notes paraphrased for demonstration, not real quotes.
Figure 2. Thematic analysis, from transcripts to themes. Illustrative, synthetic counts.

Insight

Four themes explained the risk, and they converged on one story: access is granted broadly, shared freely, not tracked, and rarely revoked. Businesses default to full access because partial roles do not match how they work, agencies and ex-staff keep access long after they should, admin lists grow past the point anyone reviews them, and clean-up only happens after something breaks. The risk is structural and behavioural, not just technical.

Figure 3. Thematic map, four themes, one story. Illustrative.

Impact, what moved

  • Produced a research-backed set of recommendations, tenure-gated sensitive actions, scoped and expiring access for agencies, change notifications, and clearer admin management, that informed the team owning the surface.
  • Several directions are being built into a new single-owner account model from the start, rather than retrofitted onto the surface being replaced, the highest-leverage outcome for durable risk reduction.
  • Reframed business-account compromise as an access-governance problem, not only an attacker-sophistication problem, shifting where the team invests.
